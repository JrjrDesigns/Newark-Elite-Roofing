Growing Community, Aging Roofs

Pataskala's Subdivisions Are Hitting Their Roofing Window

Pataskala has been one of the fastest-growing communities in central Ohio for two decades. The subdivisions that went up between 1998 and 2015 — and there are a lot of them along the US-33 corridor and the streets branching off Broad Street — are now entering the 15-to-25 year range where roofing decisions become relevant.

Builder-grade shingles installed during rapid development cycles in the 2000s were often at the lower end of the product spectrum. Combined with Ohio's hail exposure and freeze-thaw cycles, many of these roofs are showing wear ahead of schedule.

We see a lot of this in Pataskala's newer subdivisions: homes built between 2003 and 2012 with standard three-tab or entry-level architectural shingles that are granule-bare in spots and starting to curl at the edges. The homeowner bought the house three years ago and assumed the roof was fine because the home was 'newer.' Fifteen-year-old builder-grade shingles in Ohio are not fine. They're almost done.
May 1997 Windstorm

Pataskala Has a History with High-Wind Events

On May 3, 1997, thunderstorms produced winds up to 70 mph specifically around Pataskala — knocking down numerous trees and causing structural damage throughout the area. That kind of high-wind event is a reminder that Pataskala's position in eastern Licking County puts it in the path of systems moving northeast from Columbus along the I-70 and US-33 corridors.

Central Ohio sees straight-line wind events capable of lifting shingles and damaging rooflines most years. After any event with reported winds over 50 mph, a free inspection is worth scheduling before the next rain.

When customers in Pataskala call us after a wind event, we often find shingles that look fine from the street but have broken adhesive seals along the lower edge. Water doesn't get in immediately — but the next significant rain, or the next winter's ice, finds the gap. Wind damage that isn't visible from the driveway is the kind that shows up as a ceiling stain six months later.
