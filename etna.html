I-70 Corridor Growth

Etna's Rapid Growth Brings New Homes — and New Roofing Questions

Etna Township has experienced some of the fastest residential growth in Licking County, driven by its position along the I-70 corridor between Columbus and Newark. The subdivisions along Broad Street, Mink Street, and the streets branching toward Pataskala have added hundreds of homes over the past decade — many of them now in the 8-to-15 year range where the first roofing questions start to emerge.

New construction doesn't mean problem-free. Builder-grade shingles installed during rapid development phases are often at the entry level of the product spectrum, and installation quality varies when crews are moving fast. A professional inspection identifies any installation issues before warranties expire.

We see a pattern in Etna's newer subdivisions that we see in fast-growth communities everywhere: homes that look pristine from the curb but have minor installation issues that become visible over time. Improper nail placement, insufficient flashing at step joints, ridge cap gaps — these are things that show up as small leaks 8 to 12 years in. A free inspection catches them early.
Storm Exposure

Etna's Open Terrain Gets the Full Force of Central Ohio Storms

Etna's relatively flat, open terrain along the I-70 corridor means homes here get the full force of storms moving northeast from Columbus. Central Ohio sees documented hail and high-wind events most years — and the newer subdivisions in Etna are fully exposed to systems that older, tree-lined neighborhoods absorb partially.

After any storm where hail is reported in the Etna or Pataskala area, scheduling a free inspection is worth doing. Hail damage on newer homes can accelerate significantly in subsequent seasons if left unaddressed.

When customers in Etna ask about their roof after a storm, we usually say the same thing: the fact that the shingles are newer doesn't mean they weren't affected. Hail doesn't discriminate by age. We've found significant impact damage on 4-year-old roofs and perfectly intact 18-year-old roofs on the same street, depending on where the hailstone pattern tracked.
